# What the Rental Housing Act Actually Requires in a Lease

Most landlord-tenant disputes in South Africa come down to one thing: the lease. Either it didn't exist in writing, or it left out something the Rental Housing Act (Act 50 of 1999) requires. When a tenant takes a matter to the Rental Housing Tribunal, a defective lease is often the landlord's weakest point.

Getting your lease right costs nothing extra. Getting it wrong can cost you months of lost rent, deposit disputes, or a Tribunal ruling that goes against you.

Here is what the RHA actually requires.

---

## Why a Written Lease Matters

Section 5 of the RHA states that either party may require a lease to be in writing. In practice, this means a verbal agreement is technically enforceable — but trying to prove its terms before a Tribunal is a different matter entirely. Any landlord managing a property professionally should have a written lease, signed by both parties, before a tenant moves in.

A signed written lease is also your primary evidence in deposit disputes, damage claims, and early-cancellation matters.

---

## The 8 Things the RHA Requires in a Written Lease

Section 5(2) of the RHA sets out what a written lease must contain. These are not optional.

**1. Full names and addresses of both parties**
The landlord's full legal name (or registered entity name) and the tenant's full name, along with their respective addresses for correspondence and legal notices.

**2. The address of the dwelling**
A clear physical description of the property being let, including the unit number if applicable.

**3. The monthly rental amount**
The agreed monthly rent in rand, stated clearly. If the rent escalates annually, the escalation percentage or formula must be specified.

**4. The payment date and method**
When rent is due each month (e.g., the 1st) and how it must be paid. Many landlords now include the bank account details and the unique payment reference in this clause.

**5. The lease period**
The start date, end date (or duration), and what happens at the end of the period — whether it converts to a month-to-month arrangement or terminates automatically.

**6. The deposit amount**
If a deposit is taken, the lease must state the amount. The RHA (section 5(3)) further requires the deposit to be invested in an interest-bearing account for the duration of the tenancy, and the interest belongs to the tenant. This is one of the most commonly contested clauses at Tribunal.

**7. Notice period for cancellation**
How much notice either party must give to cancel the lease. For fixed-term leases, this is governed by the Consumer Protection Act (CPA) as well — a tenant on a fixed-term lease may cancel with 20 business days' written notice, subject to a reasonable penalty.

**8. The landlord's obligations**
The RHA requires that the dwelling be reasonably maintained and that the tenant's right to undisturbed use and enjoyment of the property be respected. These obligations should be stated in the lease, not assumed.

## Common Clauses Landlords Miss or Get Wrong

Beyond the section 5(2) requirements, experienced property practitioners know that certain clauses are frequently the source of disputes — and are regularly absent or poorly worded in template leases.

**Deposit investment obligation.** Many leases state the deposit amount but say nothing about investing it. The RHA is explicit: the deposit must be invested in an interest-bearing account at a bank registered under the Banks Act. At the end of the tenancy, the landlord must provide proof of the interest earned. Omitting this invites a Tribunal complaint.

**Inspection clauses.** The RHA requires an incoming and outgoing inspection to be conducted jointly with the tenant, with a written record signed by both parties. A lease that does not reference the inspection process leaves the landlord without grounds to claim damages from the deposit.

**Maintenance responsibility split.** The lease should clearly distinguish between the landlord's structural maintenance obligations and the tenant's day-to-day upkeep responsibilities (e.g., keeping drains clear, replacing light bulbs). Vague language here leads to disputes.

**Utility and municipal account responsibility.** Who is liable for water, electricity, and refuse removal? If the tenant pays directly, the account should be in their name. If the landlord accounts and recoups, the recovery mechanism must be stated. Tribunal disputes over utility arrears are common where this is left to verbal understanding.

**Pets and alterations.** The lease must state whether pets are permitted and whether the tenant may make any alterations or improvements to the property. Silence here is not protection — it is ambiguity that typically resolves in the tenant's favour at Tribunal.
