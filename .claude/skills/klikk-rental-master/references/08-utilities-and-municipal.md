# Utilities and Municipal Accounts

Who pays for what, how municipal accounts work, and landlord vs tenant responsibilities for utilities in South African rentals.

---

## Fundamental Principle

The **registered property owner** is ultimately liable to the municipality for all rates and services on the property — regardless of any arrangement with the tenant. If the tenant doesn't pay, the municipality pursues the **owner**.

---

## Municipal Account Components

| Component | Description | Typically paid by |
|---|---|---|
| **Property rates** | Annual property tax based on municipal valuation | Landlord (included in rent) |
| **Refuse removal** | Waste collection service | Landlord (included in rent) |
| **Sewerage** | Wastewater treatment | Landlord (included in rent) |
| **Water** | Metered consumption | Tenant (per lease terms) |
| **Electricity** | Metered consumption or prepaid | Tenant (per lease terms) |

**Important**: The lease agreement must clearly specify who pays for each utility. Ambiguity leads to disputes.

---

## Responsibility Models

### Model 1: Tenant Pays Consumption (Most Common)
- Landlord covers fixed costs (rates, refuse, sewerage)
- Tenant pays metered consumption (water, electricity)
- Meter readings taken at move-in and move-out
- Tenant pays based on actual usage
- **Best for**: Stand-alone houses, units with separate meters

### Model 2: All-Inclusive Rent
- All utilities included in the monthly rental amount
- Provides predictability for the tenant
- Landlord bears risk of high consumption
- **Best for**: Furnished/serviced apartments, short-term lets

### Model 3: Prepaid Meters
- Tenant purchases prepaid tokens for electricity (and sometimes water)
- No monthly billing — pay-as-you-go
- Landlord has no exposure to consumption costs
- **Best for**: Multi-unit properties, high-risk tenancies

### Model 4: Hybrid
- Some utilities included (e.g., water up to a cap), others tenant's responsibility
- Must be clearly specified in the lease with caps and overage rates
- **Best for**: Properties where one utility is hard to separate

## Electricity

### Prepaid Meters (Recommended for Rentals)
- **Municipal prepaid**: Apply at local municipality or Eskom
- **Private prepaid**: Installed by landlord or body corporate, managed by private company
- Tenant buys tokens at retail outlets, online, or via apps
- No monthly bill — eliminates arrears risk
- Meter number must be provided to tenant at move-in

### Conventional (Billed) Meters
- Municipality or Eskom reads meter monthly and sends bill
- Account must be in **property owner's name** (most municipalities)
- Some municipalities allow account in tenant's name (check locally)
- Landlord liable for arrears if tenant defaults

### Key Rules
- Landlord **may NOT charge more** than the actual municipal/Eskom tariff
- Landlord **may NOT disconnect electricity** for non-payment of rent — this is an unfair practice (RHA s4A) and potentially criminal (PIE s8)
- Landlord may disconnect only if tenant has not paid for the electricity itself AND only through proper legal channels

---

## Water

### Metered Water
- Almost always **postpaid** — billed monthly by municipality
- Account in property owner's name (most municipalities)
- Meter reading at move-in and move-out establishes tenant's consumption period
- Landlord liable if tenant defaults

### Prepaid Water (Less Common)
- Some newer developments have prepaid water meters
- Same token system as prepaid electricity

### Water Restrictions
- Western Cape and other drought-prone areas may impose water restrictions
- Lease should address tenant obligations during restrictions
- Excessive usage fines may apply — specify responsibility in the lease

### Key Rules
- Landlord **may NOT cut water** for rent non-payment
- Landlord is responsible for maintaining water infrastructure (pipes, taps, geyser)
- Tenant responsible for reporting leaks promptly

---

## Municipal Deposits

Separate from the rental security deposit:
- Municipalities may require a **utility deposit** from the account holder (property owner)
- Amount varies by municipality and property type
- Refundable when account is closed
- This is the **landlord's cost**, not the tenant's (unless lease specifies otherwise)

## Account Management at Tenancy Change

### Move-In
1. Take meter readings (electricity and water) — photograph the meters with date
2. Record readings in the lease or inspection report
3. Notify municipality of new occupant (if required locally)
4. Provide tenant with prepaid meter numbers (if applicable)
5. Ensure all services are connected and functional

### Move-Out
1. Take final meter readings — photograph with date
2. Calculate outstanding consumption
3. Settle any outstanding amounts (deduct from deposit if necessary)
4. Notify municipality of vacancy (prevents ongoing billing)
5. If account is in tenant's name, ensure proper closure

---

## Landlord Cannot Disconnect Utilities

This point bears repeating because it is the most common utility-related violation:

| Action | Legal status |
|---|---|
| Cutting electricity for rent arrears | **Unlawful** — unfair practice (RHA s4A) |
| Cutting water for rent arrears | **Unlawful** — unfair practice (RHA s4A) |
| Removing prepaid meter | **Unlawful** |
| Refusing to provide meter number/tokens | **Unlawful** |
| Blocking utility access | **Unlawful** — may constitute constructive eviction |

**Tenant's remedy**: File complaint with Rental Housing Tribunal (free) or obtain urgent court interdict.

**Landlord's remedy for non-payment**: Follow proper legal process — breach notice → cancellation → PIE Act eviction if necessary.

---

## Body Corporate Utilities (Sectional Title)

- Body corporate may manage bulk utility supply and sub-meter to units
- Individual unit consumption billed via levy account or separate utility bill
- Body corporate cannot disconnect individual units without proper process
- Owner remains liable for unit's utility consumption to the body corporate
- Pre-paid sub-meters increasingly common in complexes

---

## Practical Tips

**For landlords:**
- Install prepaid electricity meters where possible — eliminates arrears risk
- Take dated photographs of meter readings at every tenant change
- Include clear utility responsibility clauses in the lease
- Budget for rates, refuse, and sewerage as part of your ownership costs
- Never disconnect utilities as a pressure tactic — it will backfire legally

**For tenants:**
- Request meter readings at move-in and keep a copy
- Keep records of all utility payments and token purchases
- Report water leaks immediately — you may be liable for excessive consumption if you don't
- If landlord threatens to cut utilities, contact the Rental Housing Tribunal immediately
