# Eviction and the PIE Act

The Prevention of Illegal Eviction from and Unlawful Occupation of Land Act 19 of 1998 (PIE Act) governs ALL evictions in South Africa. No eviction may occur without a court order.

---

## Fundamental Rule

**A landlord may NEVER evict a tenant without a court order.** Self-help eviction — changing locks, cutting water/electricity, removing belongings, intimidating the occupier — is a **criminal offence** under PIE s8 and an **unfair practice** under RHA s4A.

---

## Types of Occupiers

| Type | Description | Eviction route |
|---|---|---|
| **Tenant** | Lawful occupier with a valid lease | Cancel lease first (breach/notice) → then PIE Act court application |
| **Holding-over tenant** | Lease expired, tenant refuses to leave | PIE Act court application |
| **Unlawful occupier** | Person without consent or legal right | PIE Act s4 court application |
| **Former owner** | Lost property through sale/foreclosure | PIE Act s5 (slightly different procedure for mortgage default) |

---

## Eviction Process — Step by Step

### Step 1: Terminate the Right of Occupation

Before applying to court, the landlord must first **lawfully terminate** the lease or occupation right:

**For breach of lease:**
1. Serve written breach notice specifying the breach
2. Allow 20 business days to remedy (CPA requirement)
3. If not remedied, serve written cancellation notice
4. Lease is now cancelled — occupier becomes unlawful

**For expiry of fixed-term:**
1. Lease expires naturally
2. If tenant does not vacate → occupier becomes unlawful

**For month-to-month:**
1. Serve 1 calendar month's written notice
2. If tenant does not vacate after notice period → occupier becomes unlawful

### Step 2: Court Application under PIE Act

1. **Prepare court papers**: Notice of Motion and founding affidavit setting out:
   - Description of property
   - Basis of occupation (lease details)
   - How occupation became unlawful (cancellation, expiry, etc.)
   - Efforts to resolve the matter
   - Duration of occupation
   - Personal circumstances of occupier (if known)

2. **Serve PIE notice**: At least **14 calendar days** before the hearing date
   - On the occupier (personally or at the property)
   - On the **municipality** (mandatory — PIE s4(2))
   - Notice must inform occupier of the right to appear and oppose

3. **Municipality notification**: The municipality may intervene if the occupier has no alternative accommodation

### Step 3: Court Hearing

The court applies the **"just and equitable"** test (PIE s4(6)–(7)):

| Factor | Consideration |
|---|---|
| Duration of occupation | Longer occupation → more protection |
| Circumstances of occupier | Elderly, disabled, children, female-headed households receive extra consideration |
| Availability of alternative accommodation | Court may delay eviction if no alternatives exist |
| Rights of the property owner | Balanced against occupier's rights |
| Whether municipality can provide emergency housing | Municipality's obligations under s26 of the Constitution |

**For occupation > 6 months**: Court must consider all relevant circumstances including whether land has been made available by the municipality or other organ of state.

**For occupation < 6 months**: Slightly lower threshold, but court still applies just and equitable test.

### Step 4: Court Order

If granted, the court order specifies:
- Date by which occupier must vacate
- That the Sheriff of the Court may execute the eviction if occupier does not comply
- Any conditions (e.g., grace period, alternative accommodation arrangements)

### Step 5: Execution by Sheriff

- If occupier does not vacate by the specified date, the **Sheriff** carries out the eviction
- Only the Sheriff may physically remove the occupier and their belongings
- Landlord may NOT participate in the physical eviction
- Sheriff must act within the terms of the court order

---

## What Landlords CANNOT Do

| Prohibited action | Consequence |
|---|---|
| Change locks | Criminal offence (PIE s8); tenant can get spoliation order |
| Cut water supply | Criminal offence; unfair practice (RHA s4A) |
| Cut electricity supply | Criminal offence; unfair practice (RHA s4A) |
| Remove tenant's belongings | Criminal offence; theft charges possible |
| Board up windows/doors | Criminal offence |
| Intimidate or threaten | Criminal offence; possible harassment charges |
| Hire private security to remove tenant | Criminal offence — only Sheriff may execute |
| Enter property without notice | Violation of tenant's right to privacy |

**Spoliation order**: If landlord uses self-help, tenant can obtain an urgent spoliation order from court restoring their possession — even if the tenant is in breach of the lease.

---

## Urgent Eviction Proceedings (PIE s5)

Available in limited circumstances:
- Occupation began **less than 6 months ago**
- Property was invaded (land invasion)
- **Imminent danger** to persons or property
- Court may shorten the notice period but must still apply just and equitable test

## Typical Timeline

| Stage | Estimated duration |
|---|---|
| Breach/cancellation notice | 20 business days |
| Preparation of court papers | 1–2 weeks |
| Service of PIE notice | 14 days minimum |
| Court hearing | 1–3 months (depends on court roll) |
| Court order grace period | 1–4 weeks (court's discretion) |
| Sheriff execution | 1–2 weeks after grace period |
| **Total (typical)** | **3–6 months from first notice** |

**Note**: Complex cases (vulnerable occupiers, opposing papers filed) can take significantly longer.

## Alternatives to Eviction

| Alternative | When to use |
|---|---|
| Negotiated departure | Tenant agrees to leave voluntarily by a set date (put in writing) |
| Cash-for-keys | Landlord offers financial incentive for tenant to vacate quickly |
| Rental Housing Tribunal | For non-eviction disputes (deposit, maintenance, unfair practices) |
| Mediation | Private or court-annexed mediation to reach agreement |

**Remember**: The Tribunal **cannot order evictions** — that requires a court application under PIE.

---

## Special Situations

### Tenant Sub-lets Without Consent
- Landlord's remedy is against the tenant (breach of lease)
- Sub-tenant may also be considered an unlawful occupier if main tenant's lease is cancelled
- PIE Act protections still apply to sub-tenant

### Deceased Tenant
- Lease does not automatically terminate on death
- Estate executor steps into tenant's shoes
- Surviving family members occupying the property have rights under PIE

### Domestic Violence
- Protection from Domestic Violence Act may grant occupation rights to the victim
- Court protection order may prevent eviction of domestic violence victim
- Specialist legal advice required
