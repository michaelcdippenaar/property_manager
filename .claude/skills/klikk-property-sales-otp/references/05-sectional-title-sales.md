# Sectional Title Sales — Levy Clearance, EUAs, STSMA & Body Corporate

## 1. Legislative Framework

| Act | Role in Sectional Title Sales |
|---|---|
| **Sectional Titles Act 95 of 1986 (STA)** | Creation, registration, and transfer of sectional title units; administered through the Deeds Office |
| **STSMA 8 of 2011** | Management of schemes: body corporate functions, levies, prescribed management rules (PMR) and conduct rules (PCR); administered through CSOS |
| **STA Amendment Act 33 of 2013** | Updated scheme administration provisions |
| **STA Amendment Act 13 of 2022** | Further amendments to unit boundaries and plan requirements |

---

## 2. Levy Clearance Certificate — STA s15B(3)(a)(i)(aa)

This is the primary mechanism preventing transfer of a sectional title unit unless the
body corporate certifies that all levies are paid.

### The Requirement

Before the Deeds Office registers a transfer, the **body corporate must certify** that:

> *"all moneys due to the body corporate by the transferor in respect of the said unit
> have been paid, or that satisfactory provision has been made for payment thereof."*

The certificate is a prerequisite for lodgement — no certificate, no registration.

### What the Clearance Figure Includes

- Normal monthly levies in arrears up to estimated transfer date
- Special levies raised prior to the transfer date
- Interest on arrears
- Legal costs incurred by the body corporate in collecting arrears
- CSOS levies (Community Schemes Ombud Service levy — STSMA s59)
- Judgment debts against the owner for body corporate amounts

### What the Clearance Figure Does NOT Include

- Personal debts of the owner unrelated to levies
- Personal legal costs owed by the owner to a third party
- Future levies after the transfer date (these become the buyer's responsibility)

### Body Corporate Cannot Withhold for Non-Financial Reasons

The body corporate may only withhold the clearance certificate for financial reasons.
It is **unlawful** to withhold for:
- Conduct rule violations
- Non-submission of building plans or renovation approvals
- Personal disputes between the seller and trustees
- Any non-financial reason

*Body Corporate Marsh Rose v Stein Muller* confirmed that withholding for non-financial
reasons can result in punitive cost orders against the body corporate.

### Special Levy Apportionment Under the STSMA

This is a major change from the old STA regime:

- **Old STA:** The seller had to settle the full outstanding special levy before transfer
- **STSMA (current):** If a special levy is still being collected at the date of transfer,
  the **buyer becomes liable pro rata from the transfer date onward**. Only arrears up
  to the transfer date remain the seller's obligation

**Critical OTP clause:** The OTP must explicitly address how any in-progress special levy
is apportioned between buyer and seller. Failure to do so is a common source of
post-transfer disputes — the buyer unexpectedly inherits ongoing special levy payments.

### Practical Process

1. Conveyancer requests clearance figures from body corporate (typically valid 30–60 days)
2. Seller (or conveyancer from seller's proceeds) pays the outstanding amount
3. Alternatively, the conveyancer provides an **irrevocable undertaking** to pay from
   sale proceeds upon registration
4. Body corporate issues the s15B(3) certificate to the conveyancer for Deeds lodgement

## 3. Pre-Purchase Investigation Obligations

There is no statutory pre-purchase disclosure checklist equivalent to the UK's TA6
form for sectional title. Buyers and their advisors should investigate:

**From the body corporate (request formally via the agent/conveyancer):**
- Minutes of recent AGMs and trustee meetings (reveal planned special levies, pending
  litigation, deferred maintenance)
- Current levy schedule and monthly levy amount
- Reserve fund balance and recent financial statements
- Copy of current management rules (PMR) and conduct rules (PCR)
- Outstanding insurance claims
- Any pending CSOS adjudication or litigation involving the scheme

**From the Deeds Office:**
- Sectional plan — verify unit boundaries, common property, and registered exclusive use areas
- Title deed — verify registered conditions, servitudes, and endorsements

**From the seller (via PPA s67 mandatory disclosure form):**
- Physical condition of the unit
- Known defects in the unit (damp, roof, electrical, plumbing)
- Any pending body corporate disputes involving this specific unit

## 4. Exclusive Use Areas (EUAs) — STA ss27, 60, 75

EUAs are one of the most common sources of post-transfer disputes in sectional title sales.

### Two Types of EUAs — Know the Difference

| Type | Registration | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| **Registered EUA** (real right) | Registered in the Deeds Office as a notarial cession or part of the sectional plan | Binding on all successors; transfers automatically with or by cession of the unit |
| **Conduct-rule EUA** (informal allocation) | Allocated by the body corporate via conduct rules (parking bays, garden areas informally assigned) | **NOT a registered real right** — body corporate can reallocate or withdraw them; do NOT automatically transfer to a buyer |

### The Common Pitfall

A seller represents a parking bay or garden as "belonging to the unit" when it is
only a conduct-rule allocation. The buyer cannot enforce this against the body
corporate after transfer. The OTP must specify:

1. Whether each EUA is registered (and if so, the Deeds reference / cession number), OR
2. That it is a conduct-rule allocation only, and the buyer accepts the risk of
   possible reallocation

**Transfer of registered EUAs:** If the EUA is registered as a notarial cession, the
cession must be transferred simultaneously with the sectional unit at the Deeds Office
(this is a linked transaction and adds to the conveyancing process).

---

## 5. Body Corporate Approval — What Requires It

The body corporate has **no right to approve or block the sale of a unit** — property
ownership is a fundamental right. However, body corporate consent IS required for:

- Transfer of conduct-rule EUAs (body corporate may choose to allocate the EUA to the
  new owner or reallocate it)
- Subletting in some schemes (subject to conduct rules)
- Pet ownership (subject to conduct rules — may require trustee approval)
- Renovations affecting common property or structure

---

## 6. Rates Clearance for Sectional Title

In addition to the levy clearance certificate, the conveyancer must obtain a
**municipal rates clearance certificate** from the local municipality. For sectional
title, rates are levied on each unit's participation quota (PQ) value. This covers:
- Outstanding municipal rates and taxes
- Outstanding service charges billed to the unit's municipal account

**Note:** Rates clearance covers municipal charges — it does not automatically cover
utility debts (water, electricity) if these are on a separate account in the owner's
name rather than the sectional unit's municipal account.

---

## 7. CSOS (Community Schemes Ombud Service) Levy

The CSOS levy is a mandatory levy payable by all owners in a sectional title scheme,
collected by the body corporate and paid over to CSOS. It is included in the monthly
levy statement and appears in the clearance figures. Buyers inherit this obligation
from transfer date.

---

## 8. OTP Checklist — Sectional Title Specific

Items that must be expressly addressed in a sectional title OTP (over and above the
standard freehold requirements):

- [ ] Special levy apportionment clause — how in-progress special levies are split
- [ ] EUA identification — is each EUA registered or conduct-rule? State the Deeds cession
      reference for each registered EUA
- [ ] Levy clearance certificate condition — buyer entitled to see current levy figures
      before transfer
- [ ] Conduct rules review period — buyer has X days to review conduct rules and confirm
      no show-stopper restrictions (Airbnb, pets, renovations)
- [ ] HOA clearance (if estate) — seller must obtain clearance from HOA as well as body corporate
- [ ] Occupational rent if seller remains in occupation after transfer or buyer occupies before

## 9. Common Pitfalls — Sectional Title Sales

| Issue | Risk |
|---|---|
| OTP silent on special levy apportionment | Buyer may unexpectedly inherit ongoing special levy payments |
| EUAs described as "belonging to the unit" without Deeds check | Buyer loses parking/garden; no legal recourse |
| Conduct rules not reviewed before OTP | Pet restrictions, Airbnb bans, renovation restrictions discovered post-transfer — no recourse |
| Scheme in financial distress | Pending special levy imminent; reserve fund depleted — investigate finances |
| Deferred maintenance by body corporate | May trigger a large special levy shortly after transfer |
| CSOS levies not included in clearance figures | Additional costs at transfer |
| Registered EUA cession not lodged simultaneously | Transfer registered without the EUA; buyer must pursue body corporate separately |
