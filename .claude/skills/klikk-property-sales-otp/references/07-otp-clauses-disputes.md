# OTP Clauses, Occupation, Breach, Voidability & Common Disputes

## 1. Occupation Date vs Transfer Date

**Transfer Date:** The date on which legal ownership formally passes from seller to
buyer, recorded in the Deeds Registry. Ownership passes **only at registration** — it
cannot be predetermined with precision as it depends on Deeds Office processing.

**Occupation Date:** The date on which the buyer takes physical possession. This may be:
- **Before transfer** — buyer moves in early, pays occupational rent to the seller
- **Same as transfer** — the ideal; often stated as "date of registration of transfer"
- **After transfer** — seller remains in occupation and pays occupational rent to
  the new owner

The OTP must specify the occupation date clearly. Failure to address this is a common
drafting error that creates post-registration disputes.

## 2. Occupational Rent

### What It Is

Occupational rent is compensation paid by the party occupying the property between the
occupation date and registration (or vice versa). It compensates the other party for
the period the occupant has possession without ownership having transferred.

### Rate

Industry standard: **0.6% to 1% of the purchase price per month**, with 0.8% most
common. The underlying benchmark is the open market rental value of the property.

**Examples:**
- R2,000,000 property: occupational rent = R12,000–R20,000/month
- R3,500,000 property: occupational rent = R21,000–R35,000/month

The rate should be agreed in the OTP as a rand amount or percentage — vague
"reasonable occupational rent" creates disputes.

### Who Pays Occupational Rent

| Scenario | Who Pays | To Whom |
|---|---|---|
| Buyer takes occupation before registration | Buyer | Seller |
| Seller remains in property after registration | Seller | Buyer (new owner) |
| Transfer delayed by seller's fault | Seller | Buyer (from agreed occupation date) |

### Utilities and Costs During Occupation

Unless the OTP specifically addresses it, an occupational rent clause does not
automatically transfer liability for water, electricity, levies, or rates. These
must be addressed separately in the OTP or an addendum.

## 3. Risk in Property — When Risk Passes

Under South African common law: **risk passes to the buyer on the date of occupation**
or on the date of registration, whichever is earlier.

Practical implication: if the buyer takes occupation before registration and the
house burns down, the loss (above insurance) falls on the buyer. The OTP should:
- Require the **seller to maintain adequate insurance** until registration
- Specify who bears risk in unusual scenarios (e.g., buyer in occupation before
  transfer but damage caused by seller)

## 4. Fixtures and Fittings — Inclusions and Exclusions

South African law uses the **annexure doctrine** — if an item is permanently attached
to the land with the intention to remain permanently, it becomes a fixture and passes
with the property. Items excluded must be expressly listed in the OTP.

**Perennial dispute items** — always address these explicitly:
- Light fittings and chandeliers
- Curtain rails and blinds
- Built-in appliances (oven, dishwasher, washing machine)
- Pool equipment (pump, creepy-crawly, cover)
- TV aerial / satellite dish
- Garden features (established plants, built-in braai, irrigation systems)
- Solar panels and inverters / battery systems
- Generator and transfer switch
- Air conditioning units
- Remote controls and spare keys

## 5. Breach Clauses and Default

### Default Notice Procedure

A party cannot simply cancel an OTP on breach. Standard procedure:
1. Written notice of breach delivered to the defaulting party
2. A **cure period** — typically **7 days** in residential OTPs (sometimes 14 days
   in commercial) — within which the breach must be remedied
3. If not remedied within the cure period, the innocent party may cancel and/or
   claim damages

### Damages Available to the Seller on Buyer Breach

If a buyer breaches and the seller cancels:
- Shortfall if the property is resold at a lower price
- Estate agent commission on the original sale
- A portion of conveyancing attorney fees (as set by law)
- Holding costs (bond interest, rates, levies) during the period the property remained
  unsold due to the breach
- Occupational rent if the buyer was in occupation

### Penalty Clauses and Deposit Forfeiture

The **deposit** (typically **10% of the purchase price** in residential sales) is held
in the transfer attorney's trust account and earns interest in favour of the buyer
until registration.

Where the OTP includes a forfeiture clause, the seller may claim the deposit as a
penalty on buyer breach. However:

**Conventional Penalties Act 15 of 1962:** Courts have statutory authority to **reduce
a penalty that is disproportionate** to the actual prejudice suffered by the innocent
party. If the seller's actual loss is less than the deposit, the court may return a
portion. Conversely, if actual losses exceed the deposit and there is no additional
damages clause, the seller is limited to the deposit.

**Practical note:** A deposit forfeiture clause must be **expressly stated** — it is
not implied.

## 6. What Makes an OTP Void or Voidable

### Absolutely Void (Void ab initio — No Legal Effect at All)

| Ground | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Not in writing** | ALA s2(1): a verbal agreement to buy land is void. No exceptions. |
| **Inadequate property description** | Property cannot be identified with certainty from the document — void for uncertainty |
| **Missing purchase price** | No determinable price = void |
| **Lack of legal capacity** | Minor without guardian consent; sequestrated person without trustee consent |
| **Common mistake** | Both parties contracted under a fundamental mutual mistake (e.g., neither knew a third party owned the property) |
| **Illegal object** | Sale of land illegal under statute (e.g., subdivision without approval under the Subdivision of Agricultural Land Act) |
| **Agent without written authority** | ALA s2(1): agent had no written authority to bind the principal — sale is void (Thorpe v Trittenwein) |

### Voidable (Valid Until Set Aside by Court or Party Election)

| Ground | Who Can Set Aside | Remedy |
|---|---|---|
| **Misrepresentation** | Innocent party | Rescission + damages, or reduction in price |
| **Fraudulent concealment of defects** | Buyer | Rescission (actio redhibitoria) or price reduction (actio quanti minoris) + damages |
| **Undue influence or duress** | Aggrieved party | Rescission |
| **Non est factum** (mistaken about fundamental nature of document) | Aggrieved party | Rescission (rare) |
| **Unaccepted offer / late acceptance** | Offeror | No contract; if seller accepts after offer expiry, this is a counter-offer only |
| **CPA rescission** (developer sales) | Buyer | Return/repair/replacement within 6 months |

## 7. Common OTP Drafting Errors

| Error | Risk |
|---|---|
| Vague suspensive conditions ("subject to a satisfactory inspection") | Courts struggle with purely subjective conditions; creates disputes |
| No deadline on suspensive conditions | Unenforceable and creates indefinite uncertainty |
| Incorrect entity description | Seller is "John Smith" but property is in "Smith Family Trust" — mismatch with Title Deed discovered at Deeds Office; causes rejection |
| Sectional title EUAs omitted | Buyer loses exclusive use of parking/garden; no recourse |
| Fixtures not listed | Disputes over what stays; seller removes items buyer expected |
| No VAT/transfer duty allocation | Unexpected tax exposure, especially for developer or trust sellers |
| Occupational rent in wrong party's favour | Drafting error that creates claims against the wrong party |
| Automatic extensions without a cap | Bond condition can extend indefinitely, trapping the seller |
| 72-hour clause missing alongside subject-to-sale | Seller cannot accept better offers during the waiting period |
| No provision for death of a party | Delays and uncertainty if seller dies before registration |
| Amendments via WhatsApp without signatures | Legally precarious; ALA s2(1) applies to all material amendments |

## 8. Common Post-Transfer Disputes

### Latent Defect Disputes

The most common post-transfer dispute in SA residential property. Classic scenarios:
damp concealed under fresh paint, illegal building structures, subsidence, water
pressure issues, roof leaks, boundary encroachments.

**Under voetstoots (private seller):** Buyer must prove the seller knew of the defect
and concealed it fraudulently — a high standard.

**Under CPA (developer):** The seller must prove the property was in good condition.
The burden shifts to the developer.

**Remedies:**
- *Actio redhibitoria* — full rescission (return the property, get purchase price back)
- *Actio quanti minoris* — proportional price reduction to reflect the defect
- Damages (in addition to either remedy)

### Late Transfer

If transfer takes longer than expected, the party in occupation pays occupational
rent for the extended period. If the delay is caused by the seller (failure to provide
documents, failure to clear levy arrears), the seller owes occupational rent from the
agreed occupation date. An interdict can be sought to compel cooperation.

### Failed Bond / Non-Fulfilment of Bond Condition

If the bond condition genuinely fails (bank declines through no fault of the buyer),
the OTP falls away with no claim from either party. However:
- If the buyer never applied: breach — seller can claim damages
- If the buyer received approval but deliberately refused to accept (to escape the deal):
  seller may argue fictional fulfilment

**Marais v Kovacs Investments:** Approval for a lesser amount than specified means the
condition failed — the seller cannot compel the buyer to accept a partial bond.
