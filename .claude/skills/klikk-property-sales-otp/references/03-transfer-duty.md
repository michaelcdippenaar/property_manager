# Transfer Duty — Rates, VAT Interaction, SARS Deadlines & Exemptions

## 1. Who Pays Transfer Duty

Under the **Transfer Duty Act 40 of 1949, s2**, the **purchaser/buyer** bears the
transfer duty obligation — not the seller. Duty is calculated on the transaction
value declared to SARS. SARS may reassess and impose the correct duty plus penalties
if the declared value appears below market value.

## 3. VAT vs Transfer Duty — Mutual Exclusion (TDA s9(15))

This is a critical rule: **a property transaction cannot attract both VAT and transfer
duty — they are mutually exclusive.**

| Seller's Status | Tax Applicable | Note |
|---|---|---|
| **Private individual** (not a VAT vendor) | **Transfer duty** at rates above | Standard residential resale |
| **Registered VAT vendor** selling as part of their enterprise | **VAT at 15%** — transfer duty **not payable** | Developer selling new units; investor selling commercial property |
| **Going concern** sale (commercial property, VAT Act s11(1)(e)) | **VAT at 0%** — transfer duty not payable | Requires strict compliance with going concern requirements |

**Practical check:** Ask the seller's attorney whether the seller is a VAT vendor at
the time of sale. Developers are typically VAT vendors; private sellers are not.
Getting this wrong results in paying the wrong tax and requires a SARS correction.

**Note on VAT in residential sales:** Where a developer sells a new residential unit
and VAT applies, the VAT is typically included in the purchase price. The buyer should
confirm with the developer whether the stated price is VAT-inclusive or VAT-exclusive.

## 4. Section 9 Exemptions

| Subsection | Exemption |
|---|---|
| **s9(1)(e)** | Heir or legatee acquiring from a deceased estate (intestate or testate inheritance) |
| **s9(1)(i)** | Transfer between former spouses pursuant to a **divorce order** (court order or incorporated settlement) |
| **s9(1)(k)** | Transfer reflecting automatic half-share acquisition in marriages in **community of property** |
| **s9(3)** | Transfer from a partnership to its individual partners upon dissolution (pro rata to each partner's interest) |
| **s9(4)(b)** | Transfer from a trust to a beneficiary who is a "relative" of the founder and pays no consideration |
| **s9(15)** | Transactions subject to VAT (mutual exclusion — see above) |
| **s9(15A)** | Asset-for-share transactions under ITA s42 where ITA s42 and VAT Act s8(25) requirements are met |
| **s9(1)(l)(i)** | Acquisition by a company in terms of an asset-for-share transaction per ITA s42 |

**Exemptions are NOT automatic** — the conveyancer must apply to SARS for an
exemption certificate, and SARS must issue it before the Deeds Office will register
the transfer without payment.

## 5. SARS Payment Deadline and Process

### Deadline: 6 Months from Date of Acquisition

Transfer duty must be paid to SARS within **6 months** from the date of acquisition.
The start date is the date the OTP was signed (or the date the last suspensive
condition was fulfilled — in practice, SARS typically uses the OTP date as the
start date).

**This is a hard deadline.** The Deeds Office will not register a transfer without a
SARS transfer duty receipt (or exemption certificate), creating a hard stop on the
transaction. Instruct your conveyancer early.

### How Payment Works

1. The conveyancer submits a transfer duty declaration via SARS eFiling on behalf of
   the purchaser
2. SARS assesses the declaration
3. The conveyancer pays the duty from funds provided by the purchaser
4. SARS issues a **transfer duty receipt** (historically "TD3") within 5–10 working
   days of eFiling and payment
5. The conveyancer lodges the receipt at the Deeds Office as part of the transfer
   documents

## 6. Late Payment — Interest and Penalties

If transfer duty is not paid within the 6-month interest-free period:

| Charge | Rate / Rule |
|---|---|
| **Interest** | 10% per annum on the outstanding amount, calculated per **completed month** after the 6-month window |
| **Administrative penalties** | Up to 10% of the outstanding amount under the Tax Administration Act |

**Example:** R100,000 transfer duty bill, paid 3 completed months late:
R100,000 × 10% × 3/12 = **R2,500 in interest** (plus potential penalties)

Common causes of delay that trigger late payment interest:
- Purchaser slow to provide FICA documents to conveyancer
- Delay in appointing a conveyancer after signing OTP
- Dispute between parties causing the transaction to stall
- Purchaser expecting bond to cover transfer costs (it does not)

## 7. Total Buyer Costs (Approximate — 2026 Rates)

Transfer duty is only one of the buyer's costs. Approximate total buyer costs:

| Property Value | Transfer Duty | Transfer Attorney Fees (excl. VAT) | Bond Registration Fees (excl. VAT) | Total Approx. |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| R1,000,000 | R0 | ~R26,000 | ~R22,000 | **~R55,000** |
| R2,000,000 | ~R35,000 | ~R32,000 | ~R27,000 | **~R107,000** |
| R3,000,000 | ~R107,000 | ~R37,000 | ~R32,000 | **~R191,000** |
| R5,000,000 | ~R327,000 | ~R48,000 | ~R41,000 | **~R440,000** |

*Figures are approximate; attorney fees follow the LPC guideline tariff.*
*Additional costs: Deeds Office fees, bond initiation fee, FICA verification fees,
municipal pre-payment of rates.*

**Total buyer costs as a percentage of purchase price:** approximately **5–10%**,
decreasing at higher price points (as transfer duty dominates for expensive properties).
