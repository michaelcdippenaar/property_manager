# OTP Essentials — What It Is, Legal Requirements & Key Legislation

## 1. What Is an OTP?

An Offer to Purchase (OTP) is a written contract between a buyer (purchaser) and a
seller for the sale of immovable property in South Africa. The moment it is signed by
both parties and all suspensive conditions are either fulfilled or waived, it becomes a
legally binding agreement. The OTP is the foundation document that drives the entire
conveyancing process through to registration in the Deeds Office.

In SA law, the OTP is NOT itself transfer of ownership — ownership only passes upon
registration in the Deeds Registry (Deeds Registries Act 47 of 1937, s16). The buyer
gains a personal right against the seller under the OTP; the real right (ownership) is
only acquired at registration.

## 2. Alienation of Land Act 68 of 1981 (ALA) — The Foundation

### Section 2(1) — Writing Requirement

> "No alienation of land after the commencement of this section shall, subject to the
> provisions of section 28, be of any force or effect unless it is contained in a deed
> of alienation signed by the parties thereto or by their agents acting on their written
> authority."

**What this means in practice:**

| Requirement | Consequence of Non-Compliance |
|---|---|
| Must be in writing | Verbal agreement is void ab initio — no legal effect whatsoever |
| Signed by both parties | An unsigned counter-offer is not acceptance |
| Agent must have written authority | Agent without written authority binds no one — sale is void (Thorpe v Trittenwein [2006] SCA 30) |
| Amendments must be in writing | WhatsApp or email amendments to price/date are legally precarious without signatures |

**Key case:** *Thorpe v Trittenwein [2006] SCA 30* — agents acting without written
authority meant no valid sale of land was established, even though negotiations were
advanced.

### Section 29A — Cooling-Off Right (Residential ≤ R250,000)

**Trigger conditions (all must be met):**
- Purchase price of **R250,000 or less**
- Purchaser is a **natural person** (not a company, CC, or trust)
- Property is **residential**

**What the right provides:**
- Purchaser may revoke the offer or cancel the signed OTP within **5 business days**
  of the date of signature (excludes day of signing, Saturdays, Sundays, public holidays)
- No reason required; no penalty attaches to the purchaser
- **Cannot be waived** — any contractual clause attempting to waive s29A is void

**Notice requirements (all three must be satisfied):**
1. Written notice, signed by the purchaser or authorised agent
2. Must identify the OTP being cancelled
3. Must be unconditional

**Refund:** All amounts paid must be refunded within **10 days** of delivery of the
cancellation notice.

**Excluded:** Commercial/industrial/agricultural property; properties priced above
R250,000; juristic person purchasers.

**Note:** Most residential sales in South Africa are above R250,000, so s29A
cooling-off is rarely available. Buyers rely on suspensive conditions (bond, inspection)
as their primary exit mechanism.

### ALA Chapter II — Instalment Sale Agreements (Sections 7–19)

An instalment sale arises where transfer is deferred while the purchaser pays in two or
more instalments over time (common in township/development sales by developers).

Key Chapter II requirements:

| Section | Requirement |
|---|---|
| s7 | Agreement must specify total price, instalments, interest rate, and transfer date |
| s9 | Seller must provide purchaser with prescribed information before conclusion: property description, outstanding bonds, servitudes, township establishment status |
| s11 | Intermediaries must provide certificates confirming title and encumbrances |
| s12 | Purchaser has the right to fulfil the seller's bondholder obligations to prevent foreclosure |
| s13 | Interest calculation limitations |
| s16 | Cancellation of instalment agreement requires **written notice of default** + **30-day remedy period** before cancellation takes effect |

Instalment sale agreements must be registered in the Deeds Office within **90 days**;
the purchaser acquires a real right upon registration even without transfer.

## 3. Voetstoots vs Consumer Protection Act

### The Common Law Voetstoots Clause

Voetstoots (literally "with a push of the foot" — "as is") excludes the seller's
implied warranty against latent defects. Under voetstoots:

- Buyer takes the property in its existing condition, including known and unknown defects
- Seller is NOT liable for latent defects they did not know about
- Seller CANNOT rely on voetstoots if they knew of a latent defect and **fraudulently
  concealed** it or failed to disclose it
- Buyer must prove fraudulent concealment — this is a high evidentiary burden
- The duty extends to "unusual or abnormal qualities" of the property

**Key case:** *Odendaal v Ferraris (422/07) [2008] ZASCA 85* — illegal structures are
latent defects; seller cannot hide behind voetstoots for known concealed defects.

### Consumer Protection Act 68 of 2008 (CPA) — When It Applies

The CPA applies only where the seller sells **in the ordinary course of business**:

| Seller Type | CPA Applies? |
|---|---|
| Private individual selling their own home (once-off) | **No** — voetstoots applies |
| Property developer selling new units | **Yes** — voetstoots void |
| Property speculator/investor with regular sales | **Yes** — voetstoots void |
| Estate agent acting as supplier of services | **Yes** (to their services) |
| Juristic person purchaser with annual turnover/assets > R2 million | **No** — CPA excluded (Schedule 2) |

**Where CPA applies (ss55–56):**
- Property must be of good quality, in good working order, and free of defects
- Seller must inform the buyer of the property's condition with **explicit written acceptance**
- Voetstoots clause is **void** and unenforceable
- Buyer's remedies: repair, replacement, or refund within **6 months** of delivery

**CPA s16 — Direct Marketing Cooling-Off (rarely applies to property):**
Provides 5 business days to cancel, but ONLY if the sale resulted from **direct
marketing** (unsolicited cold calls, door-to-door). Not applicable to the typical OTP
where the buyer approached an agent proactively.

### Mandatory Disclosure Form (PPA s67)

The Property Practitioners Act 22 of 2019 requires the estate agent to obtain a fully
completed mandatory disclosure form from the **seller** before accepting the mandate,
and to provide a copy to the **buyer** before any offer is made. The form must be
**attached to the OTP**.

This creates a written record of known defects alongside (or instead of) voetstoots.
If the seller signs the form indicating no known defects but a defect later emerges,
this is evidence of fraudulent concealment, undermining even a valid voetstoots clause.
See reference `06-property-practitioners.md` for full detail on the disclosure form.

## 4. Essential Elements of a Valid OTP

A valid OTP must contain all of the following to avoid being void for uncertainty or
non-compliance with the ALA:

| Element | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| **In writing** | ALA s2(1): void if not in writing |
| **Signed by both parties** (or authorised agents) | ALA s2(1): unsigned acceptance = no contract |
| **Identified parties** | Buyer and seller identified with certainty; match Deeds Registry records |
| **Property description** | Erf number, full address, or sectional plan number/scheme name with sufficient certainty |
| **Purchase price** | Must be certain or ascertainable from the agreement |
| **Voetstoots clause** (or its exclusion for CPA transactions) | — |
| **Occupation date** | Date buyer takes possession — may differ from transfer date |
| **Occupational rent** | If occupation before or after registration — rate and who pays |
| **Suspensive conditions** | Clearly stated with deadlines |
| **s29A reference** | Required if price ≤ R250,000 and buyer is a natural person |
| **Fixtures and fittings** | List what is included/excluded to prevent disputes |
| **Offer validity period** | Time within which seller must counter-sign — no contract if expired |

## 5. Key Legislation at a Glance

| Act | Key Role in OTP |
|---|---|
| **Alienation of Land Act 68 of 1981** | Writing requirement, s29A cooling-off, instalment sale rules |
| **Consumer Protection Act 68 of 2008** | Voetstoots exclusion where seller is a business, direct marketing cooling-off |
| **National Credit Act 34 of 2005** | Governs bond/mortgage application; s92 pre-agreement quote required before bond condition is fulfilled |
| **Property Practitioners Act 22 of 2019** | Agent mandates, FFCs, s67 mandatory disclosure form, trust accounts |
| **Transfer Duty Act 40 of 1949** | Transfer duty payable by buyer; VAT vs transfer duty mutual exclusion |
| **Deeds Registries Act 47 of 1937** | How ownership transfers (only at registration); conveyancer requirement |
| **Sectional Titles Act 95 of 1986** | Sectional title sales; s15B(3) levy clearance certificate |
| **STSMA 8 of 2011** | Management of sectional title schemes; levy apportionment on sale |
| **Financial Intelligence Centre Act 38 of 2001** | FICA obligations on agents and conveyancers; KYC requirements |
| **Conventional Penalties Act 15 of 1962** | Courts may reduce disproportionate penalty/forfeiture clauses |
